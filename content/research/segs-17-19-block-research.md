# Block research dossier — segs 17-19 (km 112-134)

> Compiled 2026-05-24 by the segs 17-19 research strand. Feeds drafting strands for segs 17, 18, 19. Mirrors the shape of `segs-14-16-block-research.md` (PR #559) and `segs-11-13-block-research.md` (PR #501).

## Cross-segment threads

**The granite-to-heath transition.** This 22 km block is the route's structural pivot from the Vézère granite valleys of the Treignac corridor into the Plateau de Millevaches heathland. Seg 17 climbs out of the Vézère valley to the western edge of Madranges (494-800 m commune elevation range) on the Treignac approach. Seg 18 drops *into* Treignac (375 m at the bridges) — the day's lowest elevation since Tulle — and immediately begins the Côte de la Croix de Pey out of the Vézère gorges. Seg 19 climbs through Lestards (803 m commune elevation) onto the open landes, where the road reaches its first plateau-grade altitude (~821 m at the climb's nominal summit, ~851 m at the genuine high point further on). The textbook boundary of the Plateau de Millevaches (after Dauzat-Rostaing: Gaulish *melo* "high place" + Latin *vacua* "empty") is crossed somewhere on this climb — Wikipedia FR is explicit that the plateau "lacks precise administrative delimitation, with boundaries varying by author and criteria," but the 2005 Atlas rhombus connects Felletin / Égletons / Nedde / Ussel, and the Lestards-to-Bugeat corridor sits inside that figure. Drafters: seg 19 *is* the entry into the Plateau, even if no single signpost marks it; the landscape change — closed Vézère gorge → open lande and peat — is the writable beat.

**The 2020 directional inversion.** Stage 12 of the 2020 Tour (Chauvigny → Sarran, 10 September 2020) crossed this corridor in the opposite direction. The peloton came from the north, **climbed Côte de la Croix de Pey out of the Lestards plateau and descended into Treignac**, then climbed back out toward Suc au May from the Chaumeil side. In 2026 the order is reversed: the route descends into Treignac from the seg 16 Monédières apron, then **climbs Croix de Pey out of Treignac toward Lestards**. The road, the climb, the descent — same tarmac, different direction. Marc Hirschi (Sunweb) won the 2020 stage solo; the decisive selection happened "on the Côte de la Croix du Pey" (Cyclingnews), where Soler, Pacher, Schachmann and the Sunweb trio Benoot, Kragh Andersen and Hirschi made the front group. The 2020 climb was the bridge climb out of the Vézère valley, not the day-defining one (Suc au May was that). In 2026, with the order flipped, Croix de Pey is the *last* of the three named Corrèze climbs before Mont Bessou, and the launching pad for the long Plateau traverse of segs 20-22. This is a writable beat for seg 18: the climb has Tour pedigree, just from the other direction. Inner Ring's 2020 preview is also explicit that "the Côte de la Croix de Pey is 98% of the Col de Lestards" — confirming the two names refer to the **same climb**.

**The Vézère as connective tissue.** The Vézère rises in the Longéroux peatland on the Plateau de Millevaches at 887 m, near Meymac (seg 23-24 — reserved). It runs 211.2 km to its confluence with the Dordogne at Limeuil, dropping to 50 m. Treignac sits at km 25 or so of the river's life — its upper-mountain stretch, in granite gorges between two dam-fed lakes (Lac des Bariousses upstream, the Treignac dam at the village). The same river runs through Lascaux, Cro-Magnon, Font-de-Gaume, Les Eyzies in its lowland chalk-and-limestone stretch 100+ km downstream. UNESCO's "Sites préhistoriques et grottes ornées de la vallée de la Vézère" inscription covers the lowland reach. Seg 18 is the river's *granite* hour; the prehistoric caves are its *limestone* hour. Same water, different geology, different millennia. Drafters writing seg 18 should not over-spend the Lascaux callback (it sits 100 km away) but a single line — *this is the river of the painted caves, here at the source country* — is the available beat.

**The Resistance corridor.** Treignac sits inside the Maquis du Limousin operational area led by Georges Guingouin (military) and Gontran Royer (MUR). In May 1943, an FFI organiser took to the maquis "and was then charged with organising different camps in the Treignac–La Celle sector" (Wikipedia EN, *Maquis du Limousin*). Bugeat suffered a documented atrocity on **6 April 1944**: German forces executed four inhabitants of the L'Echameil hamlet and arrested eleven Jewish residents, of whom ten were deported to Auschwitz. The Bugeat Maquis stèle that appears in `data/attractions.json` at km 143.64 (seg 21) is the corridor's set-piece — but the *corridor identity* belongs across the block, and the seg 18 drafter writing Treignac can name the camp-organising work in the 1943 sector without poaching the Bugeat-specific names that anchor seg 21. The Treignac–La Celle sector is the verified frame.

**The Plateau-threshold framing.** Seg 19's stub title — "Bugeat — Gateway to Millevaches" — is a **threshold-framing** title, not a literal-location title. Bugeat town centre sits 8086 m off the seg-19 polyline at the seg-19/20 boundary, then 3245 m off at the seg-20/21 boundary, then **24 m off at the seg-21 polyline ~km 144**. Bugeat is therefore properly a seg 21 town (already keyed in the corridor data); the "gateway" framing in the seg 19 stub names the *threshold beat* — the moment the route crosses from the Vézère granite uplands onto the Plateau heathland — not the literal arrival at Bugeat. The dominant on-route landmark of seg 19 is **Lestards** (12 m off polyline at idx 576/827, km ~131.6), with its thatched-roof church one of the corridor's most-photographed buildings. Drafters writing seg 19: Lestards is the literal beat; the Plateau is the threshold beat; Bugeat is the foreshadow.

## Segment 17 (km 112-120) — Treignac approach via Madranges

### Geography & geology

- Madranges bourg sits 65 m off the polyline at idx 91/442 of the seg-17 GPX, ~km 113.5. The commune occupies the southern apron of the Plateau de Millevaches, with the official Parc naturel régional de Millevaches en Limousin including it inside its perimeter. Commune elevation range 494-800 m; area 12.88 km²; population 166 (2023 INSEE, declining 13.99% since 2017); density 13 hab./km². Demonym Madrangeois / Madrangeoises. Per Wikipedia FR: the commune is "arrosée par le ruisseau de Boulou et la Madrange, deux affluents de la Vézère" — both streams drain south to the Vézère, which the seg 17/18 boundary delivers the route to at Treignac.
- Geology continues the **leucogranite Limousin basement** of the seg 14-16 Monédières — same Variscan-emplaced rock (360-290 Ma) — but the heath-and-bog *paysage* of the Monédières is giving way to a closed forest-and-pasture *paysage* on the descent into the Vézère gorges. The transition is gradual; what the rider sees is more conifer plantation (Douglas-fir / sitka spruce — the 20th-century afforestation cited by Wikipedia FR as the Plateau's *"fermeture paysagère"*) and less open lande.
- **Watershed.** The seg 16 dossier left open the question of where the Corrèze/Vimbelle/Vézère watershed crosses. Working hypothesis (unverified by Géoportail in this round, flag for verification): the watershed runs somewhere through the upper Monédières apron in seg 15-16, putting all of seg 17 already in the Vézère catchment. The Madrange stream and the Boulou both drain to the Vézère, confirming the directionality but not the precise crossing line.

### Local history & archaeology

- Madranges is small — small enough that Wikipedia FR's history section is dominated by one beat: **the arrival of Protestantism in 1898**, and the inauguration of a Protestant temple on **11 March 1900**, designed by **Adolphe Augustin Rey** (a Paris-trained architect best known for social-housing work). The temple still stands. Locals had originally been part of the Le Lonzac parish and petitioned a Brive pastor; "Un temple est construit et inauguré le 11 mars 1900." For an isolated upland Corrèze commune to become Protestant *in 1898* — a century after the Revolution disestablished the Catholic Church and a generation after the 1872 Loi Falloux — is a small but writable beat for seg 17. The reformed dynamic in Bas-Limousin is unusual and Madranges is one of its rare visible markers.
- **Église Saint-Barthélemy** is the older village set-piece (Catholic; Inventaire général du patrimoine culturel). No major dating detail surfaced in this round.
- No documented Resistance set-piece for Madranges itself. The commune sits inside the Treignac–La Celle sector (1943), but no named camp / stèle on the polyline.

### Cycling context

- No documented prior Tour de France visit to Madranges; the 2020 Stage 12 route passed through the wider Treignac corridor but did not flag Madranges. **The corridor's pro-cycling shadow is in seg 18**, not seg 17. Drafters: write seg 17 as the approach without forcing a cycling-history beat.

### Famous local people

- **Mayor Jean-Pierre Bort** (retired executive, term 2020-2026) — the kind of small-commune retired-professional mayor characteristic of the Plateau. Not famous; useful only for a "who runs the village" beat if the seg 17 draft wants one.
- No documented famous figure born or formed in Madranges. The natural draw is forward to Treignac (seg 18) or back to the Monédières figures (seg 14-15, already spent).

### Culture

- Cuisine is consistent with the wider Corrèze plateau (cattle pasture, oak-chestnut bocage, *farcidure* and *millassou* dumplings, *clafoutis*); the genuine cuisine shift comes at the Plateau threshold itself (seg 19/20), where mushroom-and-myrtille mountain food displaces the lowland chestnut.
- No documented major village festival the dossier could pin to late May / early June (publish window). The seg 17 publish day is **Sun 2026-05-31**, two weeks after the Treignac Pentecost kayak weekend (see seg 18 culture).

### Literary / musical

- Nothing distinct to Madranges. The available regional anchor is Pierre Bergounioux (Brive-born, Corrèze landscape voice — already spent in seg 14-16) and Richard Millet, neither of whom has documented Madranges material. **The frame the seg 17 drafter has** is the *approach* to Treignac — the medieval town becoming visible across the Vézère gorges. The literary register is *paysage à l'orée* again, this time looking onto the medieval town rather than onto the massif.

## Segment 18 (km 120-126) — Treignac, the Vézère bridges, Croix de Pey base

### Geography & geology

- Seg 18 is the day's pivot. The route enters Treignac at km 121.73 (Gorges de la Vézère, 134 m off polyline), passes the Pont de la Tourmente at km 121.91 (319 m off), crosses the Vézère on the medieval bridge at km 121.99 (81 m off), and passes the Église Notre-Dame-des-Bans at km 122.06 (68 m off). The Côte de la Croix de Pey begins here — at the bridge or just past it. The town sits at ~375 m elevation; the climb terminates ~821 m at the nominal summit per points-config / mycols, ~822 m per GPX argmax. Net climb across seg 18: roughly +200 m over 6 km, plus continued ascent into seg 19.
- **The bridges.** Treignac has three bridges and a footbridge across the Vézère. **The Vieux Pont (Pont Médiéval) dates from 1285** — a three-span Gothic structure built of local granite to serve the now-demolished château. It is classified Monument Historique (Mérimée PA00099910) and was for nearly 600 years the village's only crossing. The **Pont Finot** dates from 1822 (or 1824 per the *mairietreignac.fr* history page — drafters check); the **Pont Bargy** from 1840. The polyline data treats **Pont de la Tourmente** as distinct from the Pont Médiéval — and 319 m off the polyline suggests it is. The dossier could not confirm the Pont de la Tourmente as a documented Treignac feature in any Wikipedia FR / mairie / Monumentum source under that name. **Working hypothesis: "Pont de la Tourmente" is the local name for one of the secondary Treignac bridges or for a pedestrian span below the dam where the Vézère narrows between granite blocks (the *Saut du Loup* rapids reach is just downstream).** Flag for the seg 18 drafter: do not write "Pont Médiéval and Pont de la Tourmente" as if they are the same bridge — they are not — but the secondary identification needs ground-truth verification. **The Hundred Years' War sack is verified**: Rodrigue de Villandrando (the Castilian routier known as *L'Écorcheur*) sacked Treignac in **1438** to recover 1,000 écus borrowed by Jean de Comborn. The market hall was rebuilt 1484, after the sack. The Wars of Religion (16th c.) inflicted further damage; the town was reconstructed from the Renaissance onward, which is why most of the surviving granite-and-half-timbered houses are 17th-19th century rather than medieval.
- **Geology.** Treignac sits on the leucogranite basement, in a tight Vézère gorge. The granite is locally quarried — the Vieux Pont, the Maison des Gardes balcony, the Porte Chabirande gateway are all of local stone — and Treignac's architectural character is granite-and-half-timbered: granite walls, granite quoins, oak half-timbering in the upper storeys, slate or chestnut-tile roofs. The Plateau de Millevaches's "pink and ochre granites" (per tourisme-correze.com) are visible in the village walls and in the river-bed stone of the gorge.

### Local history & archaeology

- **Three liberty charters: 1205, 1284, 1438.** Treignac was administered by four consuls, established as a *ville libre*, fortified with walls, and served by three early-medieval churches (Saint-Martin, Saint-Léobon, Saint-Jean) before the present Église Notre-Dame-des-Bans displaced them.
- **The château.** Built around the year 1000 on a granite outcrop within a Vézère meander ("la presqu'île"), demolished during the Revolution. The Porte Chabirande (13th c.) is the surviving fragment of the medieval enceinte. Treignac briefly renamed itself *Treignac-la-Montagne* during the Revolution.
- **The Comborn viscounty.** The Vicomtes de Comborn held Treignac through the medieval period; the Villandrando sack was a debt-recovery operation against Jean de Comborn personally. Comborn is the source-of-power that explains why a small Vézère-gorge town has medieval walls, three charters, and a market hall worth pillaging.
- **Wars of Religion damage** is a key second beat in the town history. Treignac was Catholic; the chapel **Notre-Dame-de-la-Paix was built in 1626** *outside* the walled city specifically because of inter-confessional tension — Catholics had nowhere to pray *within* the walls (per petitfute.co.uk). The dating contradicts a casual reading that the chapel followed the Edict of Nantes (1598) — it actually marks the *settlement* of the Wars of Religion in the village, two-and-a-half decades on. The chapel's set-piece is its **twisted bell tower (clocher tors)** — hexagonal section supporting a twisted octagonal portion rotated one-sixteenth of a turn left to right, "the only example in France of such complicated construction deliberately treated fantastically by the architect" (per the Clochers Tors d'Europe association). One of ~100 such bell towers in Europe.
- **Église Notre-Dame-des-Bans** is the principal Gothic church, 13th-century, on the polyline at km 122.06. (The journals.openedition.org archaeology article gives its full archaeological context — "Église Notre-Dame des Bancs et place Marc Sangnier" — note the *Bancs / Bans* orthographic variation.)
- **The Halle** — the medieval market hall, rebuilt 1484 after the Villandrando sack, restored 19th c. — sits in the village centre.
- **Resistance.** Treignac is named in the *Maquis du Limousin* operational area; the May 1943 organising work in the "Treignac–La Celle sector" is verified. The dossier did not retrieve a specific named Treignac maquisard or stèle; **flag for ground verification** at draft time.

### Cycling context

- **The 2020 Tour Stage 12 directional inversion is the seg 18 anchor.** The 2020 stage descended Croix de Pey into Treignac; 2026 climbs it out. Cyclingnews confirms the decisive 2020 selection happened "on the Côte de la Croix du Pey" (sic, both *Pey* and *du Pey* spellings circulate) — Hirschi made the front group there with Soler, Pacher, Schachmann, Benoot, Kragh Andersen. Hirschi later soloed off Suc au May with ~25 km to go and won at Sarran. For seg 18 in 2026: the same selection-and-decisive-attack logic applies in reverse — *this* climb out of Treignac is now the first hard test of the day's back half, with Suc au May already done (seg 15) and Mont Bessou ahead (seg 22).
- **The contested climb numbers (#551, open).**
  - tdf26 `data/competition/points-config.json`: **7.0 km @ 4.4%**, Cat 3, summit km 128.99 (790.5 m).
  - mycols / climbfinder: **6.4 km @ 5.1%** (max 8.9%), summit 821 m, start 520 m, gain 316 m.
  - **2020 Tour de France official roadbook**: Cat 3, **3.8 km @ 6.1%** — the tightest ASO anchor.

The points-config 7.0 km figure is internally consistent with the local elevation file per a test assertion, but disagrees with both upstream sources. The 2020 ASO figure (3.8 km @ 6.1%) is the tightest reading and the only one with TdF roadbook provenance.

Compounding this: the road *continues climbing past km 128.99* to a higher 851 m peak at km 132.52 before the Lestards-area plateau opens. The "summit" of this multi-bump climb is structurally ambiguous; the ASO-declared crest (the road's first plateau-grade flattening) is not the road's literal high point. **Drafters: write the crest as the moment the rider enters the Plateau, not the moment the GPS hits its maximum elevation.**
- **"Côte de Lestards" = "Côte de la Croix de Pey".** The 2017 Tour du Limousin Stage 3 (Saint-Pantaléon-de-Larche → Chaumeil, Cyril Gautier win) climbed something it called "Côte de Lestards" en route to Chaumeil. Inner Ring's 2020 preview is explicit that "the Côte de la Croix de Pey is 98% of the Col de Lestards" — confirming these are two names for the same climb (technically, the Lestards version is slightly shorter — the *Lestards* name picks the village as the crest, the *Croix de Pey* name picks a roadside cross as the marker). For drafters: this is a writable naming-history beat — *the climb the locals call Lestards is the climb the Tour calls Croix de Pey*.

### Famous local people

- **Charles Lachaud (1817-1882).** Born Treignac 25 February 1817, died Paris 9 December 1882, buried at Cimetière du Montparnasse, Paris. Son of Sulpice Lachaud (notary and mayor of Treignac). **The most famous Bonapartist trial lawyer of the Second Empire.** Defended **Marie Lafarge** (the 1840 arsenic-poisoning trial — one of the first French criminal cases to use scientific toxicology evidence; verdict guilty, but Lachaud's defence became foundational legal-history material); **Maréchal Bazaine** (1873 court-martial after the surrender of Metz); **Gustave Courbet** (after the Vendôme Column demolition during the Commune); **Jean-Baptiste Troppmann** (the 1869 Pantin family-massacre case, eight murders; guillotined). His wife Louise Ancelot was the daughter of the dramatist and Académicien Jacques-François Ancelot and the literary-salon hostess Virginie Ancelot. **For seg 18 drafting: the local boy who became the era's most-followed trial lawyer is the seg's strongest local-person anchor.** Primary source: fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Lachaud; CTHS dictionary; BnF authority data.bnf.fr/13339349.
- **Marc Sangnier (1873-1950).** Lachaud's grandson. Born Paris, died Paris — but **buried at the Treignac cemetery**, in an anonymous family chapel "vast and ancient … from the outside, the chapel has no distinguishing features and appears to be a tool shed" (per Landrucimetières). His son the ethnologist Paul Sangnier (1917-1938) is buried with him. Founder of **Le Sillon** (Catholic-democratic political-philosophical journal, 1894 — formally condemned by Pius X in 1910 for unauthorised lay teaching authority), deputy for Seine 1919-1924 and 1945-1950 (Mouvement Républicain Populaire), **founder of the French youth hostel movement** in 1929 (the *Épi d'or* hostel at Bierville in Boissy-la-Rivière, Essonne, was the first; the French league for youth hostels followed in 1930). The Treignac connection runs through Lachaud — Sangnier married into the Lachaud-Sangnier family seat — and is visible in the village: **Place Marc Sangnier in front of Église Notre-Dame-des-Bans**, the Lachaud-Sangnier family house at 5 rue du Docteur Fleyssac (now the **Musée des Arts et Traditions Populaires de la Haute et Moyenne Vézère**), and Sangnier's grave. For seg 18 drafting: a verified two-generation Treignac literary/political lineage — the trial lawyer grandfather + the Catholic-democrat grandson — with both anchored to the same village in different ways.
- **Edmond Tapissier (1861-1943).** Born Lyon 14 June 1861, died Treignac 27 April 1943. French painter, illustrator, lithographer, tapestry designer. Pupil of Cabanel and Cormon at the Beaux-Arts; member of Académie Julian. **Official painter of the Marine (1916)**, **Chevalier de la Légion d'honneur (1927)**. Married Jeanne Bonin (born Treignac 1880) in July 1902 and lived in Treignac for the last four decades of his life. His most-known cartoon "Les Sirènes" (1896 Salon) and "Les arts du feu en Limousin" (1908 Salon) — tapestry designs for the Gobelins Manufacture. **Available beat for the seg 18 drafter**: the Treignac painter who designed Gobelins tapestries about Limousin industrial trades, dying in his Treignac in-laws' house in the depths of WWII. Buried where, not retrieved — flag if the seg 18 draft wants to use him.
- **Camille Fleury (1914-1984).** Local artist mentioned in the museum collection per Les Amis de Treignac. No further detail retrieved this round.
- **Pierre Rodier** (14th-century bishop) — historical, listed by *mairietreignac.fr* among Treignac's notable figures. Lightweight; useful only if seg 18 needs a deeper medieval anchor.

### Culture

- **Granite-and-water is the canonical Treignac framing.** The seg 17 stub title "Treignac — Granite and Water" is the local touristic catchphrase: granite for the village stones (Vieux Pont, Porte Chabirande, Maison des Gardes balcony, the *clocher tors* chapel), water for the Vézère (the gorges, the dam-fed rapids, the kayak heritage). The Office de Tourisme Terres de Corrèze treats this as the village's identity pair. Drafters: this is a working *paire de matériaux* framing, not a fabricated one.
- **Petite Cité de Caractère, not Plus Beau Village.** Treignac joined the Plus Beaux Villages de France association in **1989** and **lost the label in 2008** — reason not surfaced in the dossier's sources; the village instead holds the **Petite Cité de Caractère®** label. **Drafters: do not write Treignac as a Plus Beau Village**; it had the label, it lost it, the current label is Petite Cité de Caractère.
- **Kayak heritage.** Treignac hosted the **first World Championships of canoë-kayak descente in 1959** (1-2 August, 110 competitors, 10 nations, 5 events — the *founding* of the discipline as a world-championship sport). The championships returned in **2000** and **2022** (3-6 June 2022, Treignac's third world championship). Every Pentecost weekend, the village runs a kayak festival on the Vézère — three days of competition, three days of dam-controlled releases, three free concerts at the Parking des Rivières. **Pentecost 2026 is 24 May**, exactly one week before seg 17 publishes (31 May). For seg 18 drafting: a verified water-sports lineage running from the discipline's 1959 founding here to its modern apex, on the same gorge the riders' bridge crosses. The amateur cyclists' dress rehearsal (L'Agglomérée) was 5 April; the local kayakers' dress rehearsal is the Pentecost weekend before publication.
- **Kind of Belou Jazz Festival** — Treignac's annual jazz festival, third weekend of August. Off-cycle for the seg 18 publish window but a recurring seasonal anchor.
- **Medieval Banquet 2026** scheduled February 17 to April 25, 2026 per the municipal agenda. Off-cycle for publish but a local-life note.

### Literary / musical

- **The Lachaud-Sangnier museum.** The Renaissance house at 5 rue du Docteur Fleyssac was the Lachaud family seat. It is now the Musée des Arts et Traditions Populaires de la Haute et Moyenne Vézère, displaying everyday objects of the Vézère valley (marriage chests, grain chests, water wheels, carpenter tools, looms, blacksmith equipment, hemp-working gear) across three floors, alongside portraits of Lachaud (pastel by Edouard Pingret) and Sangnier and works by Tapissier and Camille Fleury. The museum is itself a writable beat: the *trial lawyer's house*, where the *Catholic-democrat grandson*'s portrait hangs above the *Limousin painter's* tapestry cartoons.
- **No documented Treignac-specific novel or poem** surfaced this round. Pierre Bergounioux (the corridor's contemporary literary voice, spent in seg 14-16) and Richard Millet (Corrèze-born novelist, sometime Treignac visitor) are both *possible* anchors but neither has Treignac-specific material this dossier could verify. Drafters who want a Treignac literary voice should reach for the **Sangnier-as-democrat-Christian-writer** angle (his Le Sillon writings, his post-war MRP speeches) or for Charles Lachaud's published *Plaidoyers* (the courtroom speeches were published as several volumes in the late 19th c.) — both are verifiable Treignac-anchored bodies of writing.

## Segment 19 (km 126-134) — Lestards, Croix de Pey crest, Plateau threshold

### Geography & geology

- Seg 19 climbs through the upper Croix de Pey grade and onto the open Lestards plateau. Lestards bourg sits at 12 m off the polyline at idx 576/827, ~km 131.6 — firmly on-route. Commune elevation range 518-894 m; the settlement is at **803 m altitude**, on the Plateau de Millevaches proper per Wikipedia FR. Commune area 18.52 km²; population 117 (2023 INSEE, +10.38% since 2017 — a rare uptick on the Plateau, where the dominant demographic story is decline). Demonym Lestarois / Lestaroises.
- **The Plateau threshold.** This is the seg the *paysage* changes. Treignac's Vézère gorge (granite walls, deciduous forest, half-timbered houses) gives way to the Plateau de Millevaches's **landes** (open heath, *Calluna vulgaris* and *Erica cinerea*), **tourbières** (peat bogs — the Plateau is *château d'eau de la France*, where the Vienne, Creuse, Vézère and Corrèze all rise), and **20th-century conifer plantations** (Douglas-fir, sitka spruce — the *fermeture paysagère* Wikipedia FR names). The boundary is gradual but recognisable: the rider crests the climb, the trees thin, the horizon opens. Drafters: this is the writable geographic beat of the segment.
- **Watershed-wise**, the Plateau is the threshold the dossier's cross-segment frame names. The rivers north of the Plateau ridgeline (Vienne, Creuse) drain to the **Loire**; the rivers south (Vézère, Corrèze, Dordogne system) drain to the **Atlantic via the Garonne**. The Lestards-area crest sits on the south side of the watershed (the Vézère runs through Treignac and onward to the Dordogne), but the **catchment-wise transition** from Vézère-immediate (gorges, fast water) to Vézère-source-country (plateau peat, slow water) happens precisely here. Wikipedia FR makes the Plateau-as-water-castle framing explicit.
- The road continues climbing past the declared Croix de Pey summit (~km 129) to a higher peak at ~km 132.52 (851 m, per the dossier's elevation analysis) before the Bugeat plateau opens at the seg 19/20 boundary (km 134). Drafters: the climb's *literal* high point is in seg 19 well past the polka-dot line.

### Local history & archaeology

- **Lestards** appears in the historical record in **1315** as a parish in the La Porcherie deanery. Around **1300**, the **Ordre de Saint-Antoine-de-Viennois** (Antonine canons regular) established a **commanderie** at Lestards — a religious community charged especially with care for sufferers of *ergotisme* (Saint Anthony's fire), the gangrenous-and-hallucinogenic disease caused by rye-grain ergot infection. The choice of Lestards is not random: rye was the Plateau's staple grain, ergotism was a recurring epidemic in upland-rye regions, and the Antonines specialised in its care. The commanderie was transferred to the **Knights Hospitaller** following a 1766 royal decree (the Antonines were absorbed into the Hospitaller order in 1776 by Pius VI). **Available beat for the seg 19 drafter**: the village's medieval foundation is a *rye-grain medical mission*, and the surviving thatched-roof church is itself rye-strawed. The connection is not literary — it's material.
- **Église Saint-Martial.** Romanesque supports and barrel vaults late 12th / early 13th century; transept vault 15th-16th c. Wall bell tower (clocher-mur) flanked by four corner buttresses on the west façade. Inscribed Monument Historique 1998, classified 2002. The polyline data passes 12 m from the bourg, so the church is *on-route* — the strongest on-route set-piece in the segment.
- **The thatched-roof claim.** Wikipedia FR is unequivocal: "la seule église de France et peut être d'Europe à être couverte en chaume" — **the only church in France, and perhaps in Europe, covered in thatch**. The Office de Tourisme Terres de Corrèze, the Tourisme Corrèze departmental site, the Tarnac local-history site, and the France-in-Photos source all repeat the "only in France" framing. **The dossier could not corroborate the brief's "one of two surviving in France" claim** — neither Saint-Aignan (de l'Aurelle, or any other Saint-Aignan) nor Bénodet (or anywhere in Brittany) surfaced as a second thatched-roof church in any source consulted. The Goschenhoppen Historians piece on rye-straw thatching and the *Building Conservation* article on 21st-century thatch are silent on a second French church. **Recommendation to the seg 19 drafter: write the Lestards church as "the only thatched-roof church in France" per Wikipedia FR and Office de Tourisme**, and footnote any "two churches" reading as unverified by this dossier. The brief's "one of two" claim is likely a confusion with the broader category of *thatched-roof religious buildings* (which includes chapels, oratories, and a handful of restored buildings in Normandy and Brittany) — not parish churches.
- **The thatch itself** is **rye straw (paille de seigle)** in the **Limousin technique**: bundles tied to joists with twisted-and-knotted straw cords, *not* fixed with metal bars as in the Dutch/British technique. The Limousin technique was practised by **Armand Klavun** (one of the last masters, restoration work with sons and collaborators). The last restoration of the Lestards thatch — date not precisely confirmed in this round (Wikipedia FR mentions a recent decorative restoration of capitals; the brief's "1977" date for the thatch rebuild was not directly corroborated this round; flag for verification). **A commemorative French postage stamp was issued in June 2015** (premier-jour 26 June 2015) depicting the church.
- **Resistance / WWII.** Lestards' Wikipedia page does not document a specific WWII incident. The commune sits inside the Maquis du Limousin operational area; no named camp or stèle was retrieved this round for Lestards itself. Bugeat's 6 April 1944 atrocity (four hamlet residents executed, ten Jewish residents deported to Auschwitz) is the corridor's documented set-piece — reserved for seg 21.

### Culture

- **The thatched-roof church is the segment's set-piece.** A 12th-13th-century granite church with a 21st-century rye-straw roof, in a 117-person upland commune on the threshold of the Plateau de Millevaches. The building is the *single most-photographed object in the corridor between Treignac and Bugeat*. A 2015 French postage stamp; tourism brochures; *patrimoine insolite* listings.
- **Cuisine shifts at the Plateau threshold.** Below the Lestards crest (Treignac, Madranges, Monédières apron) the cuisine is *bas-Limousin*: cattle and chestnut and *farcidure*. Above it (Plateau de Millevaches proper: Lestards, Bugeat, Meymac) the cuisine moves toward *mountain Limousin*: blueberries (*myrtilles*) and bilberry tarts, mushroom cookery (cèpes, girolles), rye flour and rye bread (the *Antoine de Vienne medical-mission* logic returns as a culinary trace), Bleu d'Auvergne and Cantal cheeses arriving from across the Cantal border, *clafoutis aux myrtilles* in the late-summer berry season. The cuisine boundary is not sharp — but it is observable, and seg 19 is where the *first* of the upland markers (rye and myrtille) are recognisable on the village ground.
- **No documented major festival** for Lestards itself; the Saint-Martial patronal feast (30 June, broadly) does not surface as a public event in the sources consulted. The village is small enough that the church's existence *is* the cultural fact.

### Literary / musical

- Nothing distinct to Lestards. The available regional anchor is the **Plateau de Millevaches as a literary motif** — the independent journal *journal-ipns.org* (Information / Politique / Nouvelles / Sociales) is the voice-of-the-Plateau outlet; Pierre Bergounioux has Plateau-adjacent landscape essays; the wider *néo-paysan* movement of the 1970s-90s is a corridor-cultural fact about the Plateau (the "*Plateau de Millevaches as a place to go back to the land*" generation) but the seg 19 drafter should not over-spend it — the Plateau body work is reserved for segs 20-22. A quiet beat for seg 19: the threshold-naming itself — Lestards as the village where the road *names* the Plateau.
