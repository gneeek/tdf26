# Place Voltaire: The Finish Line

The road has been coming down to this for the better part of two hundred kilometres, and it arrives, in the end, quietly: a town sign at the verge, a long straight street climbing gently between houses, the granite closing in on both sides as the open country gives out behind. Ussel does not announce itself from a distance, the way a cathedral town will. It sits on its hill between two small rivers, the Diège and the Sarsonne, at something over six hundred metres, the last lift of the Plateau de Millevaches before the land tips away west toward the Dordogne. For all that height, the rivers run to the Atlantic, the Diège carrying the town's water down to the Dordogne and so to the sea, while the Loire's own headwaters rise on the same plateau a little to the north and go the other way entirely. The divide is up here somewhere, undramatic, a matter of which ditch a drop of rain happens to choose. The finish is in the town.

The name is the oldest thing about the place, and the most fitting. Ussel comes down from a Gaulish word, *uxello*, the high one, the elevated place; the Lemovices who gave it marked the obvious, that this was high ground, and the name has carried the fact for two thousand years. There is a neatness here that a writer ought to be wary of leaning on, and will lean on anyway. A stage that has spent its whole day climbing, over the Naves and the Suc au May and the Croix de Pey, across the roof of the Corrèze at Mont Bessou, comes to rest at last in a town whose name means, simply, the heights. The riders have been climbing toward a word.

The finish, to be exact about it, is on the Avenue Thiers, the town's long main street, and the sprint is judged a little beyond it, at the Place Voltaire. The two are one straight, the same hundred metres of granite-sided road; the recorded line of the route, as it happens, stops a couple of hundred metres short of the square, a shortfall in the trace rather than a different place, and the riders will cover the gap without knowing it is there. What they will know is that, after a day with barely a level kilometre in it, the run-in is flat. The last counted climb, the Côte des Gardes, is fourteen kilometres back; from its crest the road comes down and stays down, and the final stretch into Ussel is as near to flat as the day has been able to offer. What that produces is anyone's guess, a reduced bunch most likely, thinned by three and a half thousand metres of climbing, or a breakaway that has read the day right and held to the line. Whatever it is, it will not be a whole peloton. The day has seen to that. Why a finish at Ussel should be a century overdue, and what the race and its near-misses have done in this country before, is the business of this stage's companion essay, [Poulidor Country](/entries/special-poulidor-country); this one is about the town, and the arriving.

And the sprint, if it comes to a sprint, will be judged under the eye of the strangest finishing-line fixture in France. On the Place Voltaire stands an eagle, carved in the local granite, Roman work of perhaps the third century, found long ago near a mill on the Sarsonne where it had probably once crowned a tomb. It came out of the ground without its head. The head it wears now was cut and fitted in 1804, by a sculptor named Pic, when the town raised the eagle again to mark the coronation of Napoleon, so that the bird keeping the line is eighteen centuries old from the wings down and a little over two from the neck up.[^eagle] There is no arranging a thing like that. A Tour de France will be settled, on a July afternoon, beside a re-headed Roman eagle that has stood in one square or another of this town since before the town had a name that anyone now remembers.

The eagle is the oldest of the town's keepings, but not the one this stage was told to expect. Two segments back, at the abbey of Meymac, the road left a name with the reader and a promise to redeem it: the viscounts of Ventadour, who raised that abbey to its rank in 1146, and who would, it was said, have the last word in this stage, spoken at Ussel. Here it is. The lords of Ventadour began as viscounts on a high rock to the south, a line old enough to have given, in Ebles the Singer and his court, one of the cradles of the troubadour art; over the centuries the viscounty was made a county and then a duchy, and somewhere in the Renaissance the family did the sensible thing and gave up their cold fortress for the comforts of the town. They settled at Ussel, in an elegant granite hôtel that still stands in the rue des Ventadours, and made the place the seat of the duchy's business, its justice brought here from Égletons. The house that built the abbey at the top of the climb came down, in the end, to live at the foot of it. The road has carried that family the length of a stage and the better part of seven hundred years, and set them down, as promised, at Ussel.[^ventadour]

It is a small town to have been the capital of a duchy, and it has the habit, for a small town, of being where larger things start. It was here, in the spring of 1967, that a young man of thirty-four named Jacques Chirac won an election for the first time, taking the Ussel seat in the National Assembly, a constituency the left had long held, and binding to the Corrèze a career that would not afterwards let go of it.[^chirac] He went a very long way from Ussel in the years that followed, as men do; but the first seat was here, on this high ground, in this grey granite town, and the department kept him to the last.

The town keeps, too, its own quiet roster of those who lived and ended here, and among them is a man who closes a circle the stage opened a long way back. Early in the day, down in the valley at Tulle, the road passed the workshop where the Maugein accordions have been made for a hundred years, the instrument of the *bal-musette* and, for a certain French generation, the sound of the thing itself. The accordion does not return to the route until here, at the finish, in René Limouzin, who played it: a farmer's son from a commune just outside Ussel who spent his young years a musician of the dances and the weddings, then put the instrument down for a pen and wrote, into his nineties, book upon book about the old life of this country, and died at Ussel in 2020.[^limouzin] The dance tune that started at Tulle finds, at the line, the man who played it and outlived it.

Our own four arrive too, and it is right that they should arrive together. By the careful, capped, half-imaginary reckoning the standings have kept since April, holding each of them to two kilometres a day, they have come up the route abreast for weeks, and they reach Ussel level, as they reached every town before it level, no one of them ahead. The truer count says a thing the cap was built to hide and cannot quite: that each of the four passed the whole hundred and eighty-five kilometres of this route long before the road did, by the plain arithmetic of ground actually walked, and simply kept walking, so that the line they cross here is one they each crossed, in private, weeks ago. Both are true. They arrive together at the high place, by the count that flatters them with company; and each of them, alone, got here a good while before.

The road ends on the Avenue Thiers, a little short of the eagle, and there is nothing past it but the town going on about its July. Twenty-seven times the road has come into a place, and the blog has tried to say what the place was; this is the last of them, a high granite town between two small rivers, a Roman bird with a borrowed head, a duke's house in a narrow street, and a finish line a century of the Tour somehow never found until now. The riders, the real and the imagined, are in. There is no next essay, and no need of one. The road runs out of plateau and out of places, on a flat street under the eagle, and stops, where it always meant to, at the heights.

[^eagle]: The *Aigle romaine* of Ussel is a funerary eagle carved in granite, generally dated to the Roman period (about the second or third century), found near the moulin du Peuch on the Sarsonne, where it is thought to have crowned a lost funerary monument. It was discovered without its head; the present head was added in 1804 by a sculptor named Pic when the eagle was re-erected to mark Napoleon's coronation, and it now stands on the Place Voltaire (cleaned and given greater prominence during the square's refurbishment in 2009). Sources: [Aigle romaine, Tourisme Corrèze](https://www.tourismecorreze.com/en/randonnees/aigle-romaine/18109/poi/2079541); [Ussel, Corrèze, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ussel,_Corr%C3%A8ze).

[^ventadour]: The lordship of Ventadour, seated at the Château de Ventadour south of Égletons, was a vicomté raised to a comté in 1350 and to a duché in 1578 (a duché-pairie from 1589 under the house of Lévis). Its principal towns were Égletons, Ussel and Meymac. During the Renaissance the family abandoned the austere castle and installed themselves at Ussel, in the Hôtel ducal des Ventadour (rue des Ventadours, late sixteenth century, a *monument historique* since 1932); in the sixteenth century Ussel became the administrative seat of the duchy, its justice transferred there from Égletons. The medieval line included Ebles II "lo Cantador," a patron of the troubadours and a founder of the Ventadour poetic school that produced Bernart de Ventadorn. Sources: [Château de Ventadour (Corrèze), Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Ventadour_(Corr%C3%A8ze)); [Ussel (Corrèze), Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ussel_(Corr%C3%A8ze)).

[^chirac]: Jacques Chirac (1932-2019) was first elected to the National Assembly on 3 April 1967, as deputy for the third constituency of the Corrèze, the Ussel constituency, taking a long-held seat of the left; it was his first elected office, and the Corrèze remained his political base for the rest of his career. Source: [Jacques Chirac, fiche, Assemblée nationale](https://www2.assemblee-nationale.fr/sycomore/fiche/(num_dept)/1798); [Troisième circonscription de la Corrèze, Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troisi%C3%A8me_circonscription_de_la_Corr%C3%A8ze).

[^limouzin]: René Limouzin (1926-2020) was an accordionist and writer of the Haute-Corrèze, born at Saint-Pardoux-le-Vieux, near Ussel, and a player of the dance-hall and wedding circuit in his youth before turning to writing; he published many books on the traditional life of the Limousin (among them the best-selling *Les Cèpes de la colère*) and died at Ussel. Source: [René Limouzin, France 3 Nouvelle-Aquitaine](https://france3-regions.franceinfo.fr/nouvelle-aquitaine/correze/ussel/paroles-anciens-rene-limouzin-accordeon-puis-ecriture-n-plus-finir-1657806.html).
