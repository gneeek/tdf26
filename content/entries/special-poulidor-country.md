# Poulidor Country

Chaumeil is a small place on the western shoulder of the Monédières, granite and heather and a church, the kind of village a stage race climbs through on its way to somewhere it has decided is more important. For half a century it was the somewhere. On one afternoon each summer, from 1952 to 2002, the Bol d'Or des Monédières closed a circuit of these lanes, and the best riders in France came and rode them in front of a crowd that had walked up from the valleys. Robic came, and Coppi; Géminiani, Hinault, Fignon, the whole century of the sport passing through a village of a few hundred souls. When the 2026 Tour de France climbs the Suc au May in July, it will pass within about two kilometres of the line they finished on.[^bolddor]

What none of them was, was from here. That is the quiet fact underneath a hundred years of cycling in this corner of the Limousin: the country has watched the sport with real devotion and has never once produced a champion of its own. It is a cycling parish, faithful and unrewarded, and the road that Stage 9 will take on its way to Ussel runs straight through the middle of it.

The rider the corridor claims as its patron was not, if one is strict about it, its own either. Raymond Poulidor was born at Masbaraud-Mérignat, a little over the line in the Creuse, and he belongs to the whole of the Limousin in the way a saint belongs to a region rather than to a parish church. He started fourteen Tours de France and finished twelve. He stood on the final podium eight times, second on three occasions and third on five. He won seven stages. He never, in fourteen years, wore the yellow jersey, not for a day, not for the few minutes between a time check and its correction. The French called him L'Éternel Second and loved him past the point of reason, which is a national talent of a particular kind; and at Chaumeil, on the road the race will use, he won the Bol d'Or three times, in 1963, 1966 and 1967.[^poulidor] A country that had never finished first took for its patron the most celebrated second the sport has produced. One could not have arranged it more neatly.

The actual register of Corrèze professionals can be read out in a breath. Alain De Carvalho, born in Ussel, started four Tours between 1978 and 1981 and finished three of them, never higher than forty-eighth on the general classification; he is, so far as the records show, the only man born on the road Stage 9 traverses ever to have finished the race.[^decarvalho] He went home when it was over and opened a bicycle shop at Pompadour, and revived the local grand prix under his own name, which is the sort of afterlife a cycling parish offers in place of a palmarès. Before him there had been Claude Mazeaud of Vignols, who rode for Mercier in the early sixties, on Poulidor's own team, and never started a Tour at all. After both of them the regional standard passed across the departmental boundary to Luc Leblanc, out of Limoges in the Haute-Vienne, world champion in 1994 and a boy once mentored by Poulidor; in 2025 the Tour renamed the climb to Hautacam the montée Luc Leblanc, an honour the Corrèze has never been in a position to grant to one of its sons.

What the corridor has had instead of champions is constancy. The Bol d'Or ran for fifty years. The Tour du Limousin has come through nearly every August since, the resident race to the Tour's rare and ceremonial visit, and when Paris-Corrèze still existed it ended its years at Chaumeil too, lap after lap of the same circuit, as though the sport could not quite leave the place alone. The amateurs keep it now. In April of this year some eighteen hundred of them rode the Agglomérée over forty kilometres of the exact parcours Stage 9 will use, the Suc au May included, ninety-nine days before the professionals are due.[^agglomeree] The road is ridden hard here. It is simply ridden, for the most part, by people who will not be on television.

All of which goes where the stage goes, which is to Ussel. For a town of its standing, in a département the Tour has crossed a dozen times and more, Ussel carries an odd distinction: it has never once been a stage town.[^ussel] The race has finished at Brive and started from it, finished at Tulle and slept there; it has run its riders past the turn for Ussel and carried on. The nearest the town came was 2009, when the Tour du Limousin opened with Limoges to Ussel, a hundred and fifty-nine kilometres settled in a sprint won by a Slovenian most of the roadside had never heard of: a small rehearsal, seventeen years early, on the very same finish.[^limousin] On 12 July 2026 the Tour de France will come up the Avenue Thiers and decide Stage 9 in Ussel for the first time in the race's history.

The rider the parish would most have wanted to see it has been gone since 2019; Poulidor, who understood waiting better than anyone the sport has thrown up, did not live to watch the Tour finally stop where he had so often only passed. It is high country up there, the last lift of the Millevaches plateau before the land tips away toward the Dordogne, and on a July evening the light stays on the granite a long while after the valleys have gone to shadow.

[^bolddor]: The Bol d'Or des Monédières, a post-Tour exhibition criterium, was run at Chaumeil from 1952 to 2002. Among the riders who appeared on its closed circuit were Jean Robic, Fausto Coppi, Raphaël Géminiani, Bernard Hinault and Laurent Fignon. Chaumeil lies about thirty-five metres from the 2026 Stage 9 route near kilometre one hundred, and the stage climbs the Suc au May within about two kilometres of the village where the criterium finished. Source: [Bol d'Or des Monédières, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bol_d%27Or_des_Mon%C3%A9di%C3%A8res).

[^poulidor]: Raymond Poulidor (1936-2019), born at Masbaraud-Mérignat in the Creuse, started fourteen editions of the Tour de France and finished twelve, with three second places and five thirds on the general classification and seven stage wins, but never wore the yellow jersey, the record behind his nickname "L'Éternel Second." He won the Bol d'Or des Monédières at Chaumeil in 1963, 1966 and 1967. Sources: [Raymond Poulidor, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_Poulidor); [Bol d'Or des Monédières, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bol_d%27Or_des_Mon%C3%A9di%C3%A8res).

[^decarvalho]: Alain De Carvalho (born Ussel, 1953) rode as a professional from 1977 to 1982 and started the Tour de France four times (1978, 1979, 1980, 1981), finishing three editions with a best general-classification placing of forty-eighth. He later opened a cycle shop at Pompadour and revived the Grand Prix de Pompadour as the Trophée Alain De Carvalho. Claude Mazeaud (born Vignols, 1937) rode for Mercier-BP-Hutchinson, Poulidor's team, in the early 1960s but never started a Tour. Sources: [Alain De Carvalho, Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alain_De_Carvalho); [Claude Mazeaud, Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Mazeaud).

[^agglomeree]: L'Agglomérée, the Tulle agglomeration's cyclosportive, was held on 4 and 5 April 2026; the organisers reported roughly eighteen hundred participants. Each timed cyclo course covered about forty kilometres of the 2026 Tour de France Stage 9 route, including the Suc au May climb. Source: [L'Agglomérée revient les 4-5 avril 2026, Tulle Agglo](https://www.tulleagglo.fr/actualites/lagglomeree-revient-les-4-5-avril-2026/).

[^ussel]: Ussel does not appear in the Tour de France host-town record before 2026. Two pages of the reference site ledicodutour.com confirm the absence: the Corrèze department page records no Ussel stage prior to 2026, and the host-city statistics table does not list the town at all. Ussel did host the Grand Prix d'Ussel in the 1950s, won by Hugo Koblet in 1955, and the Tour du Limousin on eleven occasions, but never the Tour de France itself. Sources: [Corrèze, ledicodutour.com](https://www.ledicodutour.com/departements/departements_c/correze.html); [Villes-étapes, ledicodutour.com](https://www.ledicodutour.com/statistiques/villes_etapes.html).

[^limousin]: The 2009 Tour du Limousin opened with a stage from Limoges to Ussel of 159.6 kilometres; the Slovenian sprinter Borut Božič took the opening leader's jersey and Mathieu Perget won the overall classification. It is the closest precedent for the 2026 Tour de France finish on the same town. Source: [Tour du Limousin 2009, Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tour_du_Limousin_2009).
