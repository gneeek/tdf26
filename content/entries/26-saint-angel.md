# Saint-Angel: The Last Village

::inline-figure{src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/St_angel_eglise.JPG/960px-St_angel_eglise.JPG" lightboxSrc="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/89/St_angel_eglise.JPG" alt="The fortified priory church of Saint-Michel-des-Anges at Saint-Angel, with its round presbytery tower and Romanesque apse, standing on a granite outcrop" caption="The priory of Saint-Michel-des-Anges at Saint-Angel, fortified on its granite outcrop: the round presbytery tower, the conventual range, and the Romanesque apse, on the 1840 list of the first monuments France chose to protect. The road comes down off the last of the high country into a quieter place than any it has crossed since the morning. The villages, never thick on the plateau, thin almost to nothing; the planted forest stands close and dark to the verge, Douglas fir and Scots pine; and for a few kilometres the one thing the map asks anyone to notice is a small commune on a rock above a river, which a traveller in a hurry would pass at speed and never think of again. The river is the Triouzoune, carrying the plateau's water down the long way toward the Dordogne. The rock is a piton, a granite knuckle of the old crystalline floor that has held up the whole day, and on it stands Saint-Angel, the last village before the finish.

The ground here has not changed so much as declared itself. The plateau has no edge a surveyor could draw, but a cyclist reads one all the same in the angle of the road, and below the last rise the country relents into pasture and wood without once leaving the crystalline basement underfoot. There is no soft valley of sediment ahead; the rock holds to the line, and will hold to it past the finish. What it makes of itself at Saint-Angel is this single outcrop, steep enough to be worth defending, which is why a village and, before the village, a church came to be set on it at all.

The church is older than its papers can honestly prove. A charter dated to the year 783 records a count of Limoges, Roger, and his wife Euphrasie giving a church here, dedicated to Michael the Archangel, to the great abbey of Charroux away in Poitou; the document's authenticity has long been doubted, picked at by scholars for naming dependencies that came later, and the prudent reader takes the priory as very old without swearing to the year.[^charter] The village drew its name from it, Saint-Angel out of Saint-Michel-des-Anges, Michael of the Angels. And the thing that lifts the place above the ordinary run of Corrèze parishes is not its architecture but a stroke of administration. When France first drew up a register of the buildings it meant to protect, in 1840, the very first such list, compiled under the monuments inspectorate that Prosper Mérimée then headed, the priory church of Saint-Angel was on it. A small and emptying village on the rim of the plateau holds one of the earliest monuments the country ever decided to keep.[^listing]

The building earns its listing twice over, once for its age and once for being a known quantity to anyone who looked well at the abbey a few kilometres back. It is fortified, a church made also to be held, thick in the wall, with a round tower at the corner of the presbytery; it was burned once, in 1375, in the long war with the English, and raised up again. The body of the nave is the same heavy granite, set by the same Limousin Romanesque hand, as the clocher-porche at Meymac; the abbey's essay promised the cousin a segment on, and here it stands. Later centuries gave the nave its Gothic vaults, rebuilt toward 1500, their keystones cut with the arms of France inside the collar of the Order of Saint Michael and with the devices of the local lords; the Benedictines of Saint-Maur arrived in 1657 and kept a few monks here until the Revolution, when the commune, briefly carried away, renamed itself Angel-la-Montagne before thinking the better of it. The angel, in the end, outlasted the mountain.

What Saint-Angel has never had much of is people the wider world would know. It keeps some seven hundred inhabitants where it once kept more, the patient subtraction the plateau works on every village it holds, and it has sent almost no one into the books. The man most bound up with it never lived there at all: Jean-Baptiste Poulbrière, canon of Tulle and the diocese's tireless historian, who set down every parish in the Corrèze and who described the churches of Saint-Angel and Meymac together, in one small study, in 1880, pairing the two the way the road has paired them.[^poulbriere] What the village does have, and has lately chosen to make its one modest distinction, is the dark. It is a village étoilé, one of the places that have agreed to keep their night unlit; and there is a quiet rightness, not to be pressed too hard, in a village named for the angels keeping for its single fame the stars.

For the race there is nothing in any of this. The road across the commune gives back a little more height than it asks, a few dozen metres each way over six gently falling kilometres; the last counted climb is behind, its points all paid out on the road before Meymac, and the bunch comes through doing the arithmetic of the finish and not of the hill. The four who are covering the route at their own deliberate pace come through it level, as they have come through everything lately, held abreast by the daily cap the standings keep; and by the plain count of ground actually walked, each of them passed the full length of the stage weeks ago and simply kept on. They are near the end of it now, as the road is.

Past the village the road swings back to its line and the forest folds over it again. Ahead the rock falls away, the last of the plateau goes with it, and the granite tips downward at last toward the high town between its two rivers, the place the whole long day has been aimed at. Saint-Angel does not keep the traveller. It is the kind of village one passes through, and thinks of afterwards as the last quiet thing before the noise.

[^charter]: A charter dated 783 (some sources give 785) records the donation of the church, dedicated to the Archangel Michael, by Count Roger of Limoges and his wife Euphrasie to the abbey of Charroux in Poitou. The document's authenticity has been questioned, a 1998 study noting that it names monastic dependencies of later date; the doubt falls on the charter rather than necessarily on the foundation. Sources: [Prieuré Saint-Michel-des-Anges, Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prieur%C3%A9_Saint-Michel-des-Anges); [Saint-Angel (Corrèze), Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint-Angel_(Corr%C3%A8ze)).

[^listing]: The former priory church of Saint-Michel-des-Anges was protected on the *liste des monuments historiques de 1840*, the first national list of protected monuments, drawn up under the inspectorate of historic monuments that Prosper Mérimée headed. The chapter house and the round presbytery tower were classified on 15 July 1919, and the remaining conventual buildings together with the cemetery in 2000 (base Mérimée PA00099838). Sources: [Prieuré Saint-Michel-des-Anges, Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prieur%C3%A9_Saint-Michel-des-Anges); [base Mérimée PA00099838](https://pop.culture.gouv.fr/notice/merimee/PA00099838).

[^poulbriere]: Jean-Baptiste Poulbrière (1842-1917), priest, honorary then titular canon of Tulle and the diocese's official historiographer, compiled the *Dictionnaire historique et archéologique des paroisses du diocèse de Tulle* (2nd ed., 2 vols., Tulle, 1894-1899), a parish-by-parish history of the Corrèze. Among his earlier works was *Les Églises de Saint-Angel et de Meymac : histoire et description* (1880), which treats the two churches in a single study. Source: [Jean-Baptiste Poulbrière, Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Poulbri%C3%A8re).
