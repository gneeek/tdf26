# Off the Edge

For the better part of the afternoon the road has kept to the roof of the Plateau de Millevaches, holding its height across the open granite, and then, a little past the hundred and sixty-second kilometre, it tips and lets go. The high country gives out. The heather and the dark of the planted conifers begin to fall away, and the road, which has spent the day pretending the gradient did not concern it, settles to the steepest sustained work of the run home: very nearly two hundred and seventy metres handed back to the valley in six kilometres, against a bare eighteen climbed, the largest single drop the stage has left to give.

It is, on the map, still granite, and will be granite to the finish. The plateau is a single great body of pale leucogranite, the Millevaches batholith, and the road here does not cross from one rock to another but cuts down through the flank of the same stone it has ridden over all day. The change, in other words, is not in the ground but in the shape of it. What ends at this lip is not a geology but a height: the high impermeable roof that holds the rain where it falls, and with it the peat and the bog, gives way to steeper, better-drained, wooded valley sides. The plateau, which has no precise border anywhere on a survey, declares one here all the same, in the only language a cyclist reads, which is the angle of the road. Past this lip the tableland is finished, and though the granite stays underfoot all the way to the line, the high level country that has defined the day is, from here, simply gone.

For the race this is the most useful piece of road in the closing hour, and the riders will know it to the metre. A long descent is an honest thing, after the deceits of the high roof; it asks no more than nerve and a willingness to stay low, and it pays in the one currency the day is short of, which is time. The drop averages something under five per cent across its six kilometres, which is no Alpine wall, but it is steady, and it is long, and it comes at the hour when the legs are least able to answer it. After ninety kilometres of climbing and the high crossing behind them, a rider with the legs and the temperament to descend can take seconds here that a climber will struggle to reclaim, and the bunch, strung into a long thin line by the speed, arrives at the foot of the drop already sorted into those who mean to contest the finish and those who are merely going to reach it. The roads of Haute-Corrèze fall in long clean curves, the sightlines mostly open; the danger is not the technical difficulty but the speed, and the knowledge, sitting at the back of every mind in the line, that the day's last categorised climb is waiting at the bottom.

And the country, as the road falls, begins to soften and to fill. The austere high heath of the roof, all bog and bracken and wind, gives way by degrees to a greener and more inhabited land: pasture first, then field edges, then the scattered granite farms of the commune of Ambrugeat off to the west, the lac de Sèchemailles held in the woods below them. For a kilometre or two the road runs down through a corridor of planted conifer toward a valley that opens green at the foot of the trees. The plateau has held the riders at arm's length the whole stage, near nine hundred metres and indifferent; now, dropping below seven hundred, it relents, and lets them back down among people.

The four who are covering this route at their own pace have, by the reckoning the standings keep, drawn level. All four now stand together at the hundred and sixty-eighth kilometre, the mark that closes this descent, with seventeen still between them and Ussel. The daily figures beneath that level surface are a good deal less tidy. Nan and Justin have put in the heaviest of the recent days, averaging close to eight and to seven kilometres over the last five logged; Wally and Marian have gone more quietly, nearer three and nearer two, Marian's totals still carrying the cost of a recent crossing from Scotland to British Columbia. The standings show them abreast; the legs, as ever, do not agree.

There is a third ledger that neither the cap nor the recent averages record, and it is the most quietly remarkable of them. The route from Malemort to Ussel measures a hundred and eighty-five kilometres; by the plain count of distance walked, every one of the four passed that mark weeks ago and simply kept going.

Justin crossed the full distance in the middle of May and has walked more than a further stage-length since; Wally, the last to the mark, reached it only ten days ago. Between the head of that table and the stage itself lie better than two hundred kilometres of road the standings, holding everyone to two a day, decline to count. The contest along the route is a careful fiction; the walking beneath it is not.

At the foot of the descent the plateau is behind them for good. The road has come down off its roof, the high open ground handed back to the wind and the heather, and ahead, where the granite eases into a green basin, lies the abbey town the whole long drop has been falling toward, and beyond it the last climb, and beyond that the high town by the river that has waited a century for an afternoon like this one. The roof is ridden. What remains is the run in.
