# Mont Bessou - Roof of Corrèze

::inline-figure{src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Panorama_sur_le_Sancy_depuis_le_mont_Bessou.jpg/1280px-Panorama_sur_le_Sancy_depuis_le_mont_Bessou.jpg" alt="Panorama from the summit of Mont Bessou over forest and plateau toward the distant Puy de Sancy" caption="From the summit of Mont Bessou, the view east over the forested Plateau toward the Monts Dore and the Puy de Sancy. The stage crosses the road crest a little over a kilometre west of this point, at eight hundred and ninety-two metres." author="Lucas Destrem" authorUrl="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:Contributions/LucasD" license="CC BY-SA 4.0" licenseUrl="https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0" sourceUrl="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Panorama_sur_le_Sancy_depuis_le_mont_Bessou.jpg"}
::

Out of Bugeat the road climbs almost at once, off the granite streets and up between walls of planted conifer, and for a while there is nothing to see but the trees and the grey ribbon going up. The gradient is gentle and stays gentle. This is the climb to the highest point of the whole day, and of the whole stage, and it does not feel like one; the road gains its height the way the Plateau gains everything, slowly and without drama, a few metres at a time across open ground that has long since forgotten it was ever low.

What the road is climbing is Mont Bessou, at nine hundred and seventy-seven metres the highest point of the Corrèze, of the old Limousin, and of the Plateau de Millevaches itself. It is a curious kind of summit. The true peak, with its tower, stands a little over a kilometre east of the road, and the stage never touches it; the parcours crosses instead a broad road crest at eight hundred and ninety-two metres and tips over toward the descent. The mountain is the apex of a granite tableland, a leucogranite surface worn almost flat by long ages of erosion, and it stands above the lower country toward the Dordogne by two hundred and fifty or three hundred metres. It is, in the plainest sense, the roof.

::

For a roof it asks very little. The categorised climb is a fourth-category affair, about five kilometres at not much under three per cent, and the day's real cruelty is well behind the riders by now: the Suc au May in the Monédières, the Côte de la Croix du Pey above Treignac. By the time the race reaches its ceiling the hard climbing is done, and the highest col of the stage turns out to be the soft one. There is a small inversion in that worth pausing on. The summit a rider would mark on the profile, the one printed highest on the page, is not the one that costs the most; the ones that cost lie lower, and lie in the morning. Mont Bessou, for all its altitude, has never once been climbed by the Tour de France in more than a century of racing, and crosses into the race's history for the first time in twenty twenty-six.[^debut]

It helps to keep the altitude in proportion. Nine hundred and seventy-seven metres is the roof of the Corrèze, but it is a modest roof by the measure of the country just to the east. Two summers ago, some sixty kilometres away across the departmental line, the Tour climbed the Puy Mary in the Cantal at over fifteen hundred metres, and it was there, on the tenth of July twenty twenty-four, that Jonas Vingegaard came back from a broken body to beat Tadej Pogačar on the road to Le Lioran.[^lioran] No road joins that day to this one; the point is the contrast. Stage nine has no fifteen-hundred-metre wall. It has this gentle granite dome, the high open Plateau, and a long way still to fall to Ussel.

The mountain wears its seasons oddly too. In the nineteen seventies Mont Bessou was got up as a cross-country skiing station, with a rental chalet and, from nineteen seventy-three, a small plan d'eau, the Plateau being high enough and cold enough to hold snow when the lower valleys could not. The skiing has largely gone the way of the Limousin winters; what has taken its place is the mountain bike, and the summit is now one of the largest waymarked networks in France, a self-declared shop window for the sport in the Haute-Corrèze.[^tower] A Nordic-ski hill that becomes, in July, a Tour de France climb is the kind of thing the Plateau does without seeming to notice. Even the name is double: Bessou is the Occitan for a twin, and the mountain has one in the Puy Pendu, nine hundred and seventy metres and barely lower, standing less than two kilometres off to the north-west.

::inline-figure{src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Bessou_Tower_01.JPG/1280px-Bessou_Tower_01.JPG" alt="The wooden Douglas-fir viewing tower on the wooded summit of Mont Bessou" caption="The Douglas-fir panoramic tower on the true summit of Mont Bessou, about a kilometre and a half east of the road the stage takes; its platforms lift the visitor to roughly a thousand metres." author="Sjwells53" authorUrl="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:Contributions/Sjwells53" license="CC BY-SA 3.0" licenseUrl="https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/3.0" sourceUrl="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Bessou_Tower_01.JPG"}
::

At the crest the planted walls thin, and for a little while the road runs through open heather under more sky than it has had all day. Then it begins, very slightly, to go down, and the country it goes down into is the country where the water starts. A short way off the road to the south-east lies the Tourbière du Longéroux, the largest peat bog in the Limousin, two hundred and sixty hectares of sphagnum and sundew laid down over some seven and a half thousand years in a granite basin near nine hundred metres.[^bog] This is headwater ground; more than one river of the region rises within a few kilometres of here, on the flank of the bog and the mountain, though their sources and the abbey town they run toward belong to the next stretches of the route, not this one. For now it is enough that the watershed has been crossed. The morning's climbing tipped the riders up onto the roof; from here the long arithmetic of the day runs downhill, toward Meymac and Ussel and the finish.

The four of us keeping the walking ledger came into Bugeat dead level, all on a hundred and forty-eight kilometres of the hundred and eighty-five, not a metre between us. The roof has begun, gently, to sort us. Three cross it still locked together, on a hundred and fifty-four now with thirty-one to go; Wally, after one thin day this week, sits a touch over a hundred and fifty-two and a half, something past a kilometre adrift. It is the first daylight the table has shown in some time, and on a climb this soft it is the smallest daylight imaginable. The watershed tips the same way for all four of us, which is downhill, toward Ussel.

The summit, when it comes, is barely a summit at all, a high broad shoulder where the conifers thin and the road levels and a sign gives the altitude. The true peak and its tower stand off to the east, unvisited; the race takes the easier line, as races do. What the riders carry over the top is not the memory of a wall but the plain fact of height, the knowledge that everything from here is a falling road. The roof of the Corrèze is behind them. Ahead, for the first time since Malemort, the day is mostly downhill.

[^debut]: Mont Bessou, at 977 m the highest point of the Corrèze and of the Plateau de Millevaches, has never been climbed by the Tour de France; Stage 9 of the 2026 race, on 12 July, is its first appearance in the route. The 2026 ascent takes the gentle road crest (about 5 km at 2.7%, a fourth-category climb), not the steep cyclo-climb up the Meymac south face. Sources: [Rêve de Vélo – Étape 9 (Malemort–Ussel), parcours](https://events.revedevelo.com/etape-9-tour-de-france-2026-malemort-ussel-parcours/); [Tourisme Haute-Corrèze – le Mont Bessou, vitrine du VTT](https://www.tourisme-hautecorreze.fr/le-mont-bessou-une-vitrine-du-vtt-en-haute-correze/).

[^lioran]: On 10 July 2024, on Stage 11 of that year's Tour (Évaux-les-Bains to Le Lioran, over the Puy Mary at about 1,589 m in the neighbouring Cantal), Jonas Vingegaard attacked and beat Tadej Pogačar on the descent to the finish, his first stage win after the crash that nearly ended his season. The two days share no roads; the comparison is one of altitude alone. Sources: [Cyclingnews – 2024 Tour Stage 11 results](https://www.cyclingnews.com/races/tour-de-france-2024/stage-11/results/); [Lanterne Rouge – Stage 11 analysis](https://lanternerouge.com/2024/07/10/vingegaard-returns-to-beat-pogacar-tour-de-france-2024-stage-11/).

[^tower]: The geographic summit, about 1.3 km east of the road crest, carries a 26 m panoramic tower built of Douglas fir, inaugurated on 17 June 2005, its 148 steps and seven platforms lifting the visitor to roughly 1,000 m; on a clear day the view runs east to the Monts Dore and the Puy de Sancy, the Puy de Dôme, and the Monts du Cantal. The mountain-bike network centred on the summit is among the largest waymarked circuits in France. Sources: [Mont Bessou – Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mont_Bessou); [Tourisme Corrèze – Tour panoramique du Mont Bessou](https://www.tourismecorreze.com/en/tourisme_detail/tour_panoramique_du_mont_bessou.html); [Tourisme Haute-Corrèze – le Mont Bessou, vitrine du VTT](https://www.tourisme-hautecorreze.fr/le-mont-bessou-une-vitrine-du-vtt-en-haute-correze/).

[^bog]: The Tourbière du Longéroux (the project also renders it Longeyroux), on the flank of the mountain about a kilometre and a half south-east of the route, is the largest peat bog in the Limousin: some 259.67 hectares of sphagnum mire between about 870 and 900 m, accumulated over roughly seven and a half thousand years since the last glaciation. It spreads across four communes (Meymac, Saint-Merd-les-Oussines, Chavanac and Saint-Sulpice-les-Bois), has been protected by a biotope decree since 10 June 1986, falls within Natura 2000, and is managed by the Conservatoire d'espaces naturels; its flora includes the insectivorous sundew, its fauna otters and the short-toed snake eagle. Sources: [Tourbière du Longéroux – Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tourbi%C3%A8re_du_Long%C3%A9roux); [CEN Nouvelle-Aquitaine – Tourbière du Longeyroux](https://cen-nouvelle-aquitaine.org/sites/tourbiere-du-longeyroux/).
