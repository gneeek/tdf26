# Treignac: The Old Crossing

The previous essay left the road in the trees, climbing through plantation toward an opening it had been promised but could not yet see. The opening comes here. Somewhere above kilometre one hundred and twenty the dark ranks of Douglas fir give out, the ground tips forward, and the country that had been shut for a whole segment is handed back all at once. Below, the Vézère has cut itself a gorge in the leucogranite, and standing up out of that gorge, on its rocky spur within a meander of the river, is the town of Treignac. I confess a weakness, which I do not propose to defend at length, for towns that arrange themselves to be looked at before they are entered; and Treignac, granite and water both, has arranged itself about as well as a town of fourteen hundred souls can.

The riders come down into it, and at the bottom of the descent the road does the oldest thing a road can do at a river: it crosses. The crossing is the heart of the segment and very nearly the heart of the town. The Vieux Pont, the old bridge, is documented as early as 1285, three arches of broken span built of rubble and dressed granite to reach the now vanished château on its spur, and for something close to six hundred years it was the only way over the Vézère here, until the nineteenth century at last gave the town two easier bridges downstream.[^pont] It is not a handsome object in the way a Loire château is handsome. It is something better: exactly the thing it was made to be, of exactly the stone the gorge is made of, and it has stood in the water long enough to look less like an intervention in the landscape than a feature of it.

That granite is the first half of the local pairing, and the visitor meets it everywhere once across the bridge: in the quoins and lintels of the seventeenth and eighteenth century houses, in the thirteenth century town gate called the Porte Chabirande, in the balcony of the Maison des Gardes, all of it cut from the same pale, coarse stone the riders have been crossing since the Monédières. The second half is the water, and the water has the longer reach. The Vézère does not merely decorate Treignac; it has been the town's industry and its sport by turns. The gorge below the dam, where the river narrows between granite blocks at the rapids the locals call the Saut du Loup, is one of the founding sites of a discipline: in 1959 Treignac hosted the first world championships of canoe-kayak descent ever held, and they have come back since, most recently in 2022.[^kayak] It is a tidy thing to be able to say of a six-kilometre stretch of road, that the riders cross, on a bridge of 1285, a river that taught the world a sport in 1959.

The history that produced the bridge is the history of a small place that once mattered to larger men. Treignac belonged through the medieval centuries to the viscounts of Comborn, and a town held by a viscount acquires the apparatus of one: walls, a market hall, and, in Treignac's case, three separate charters of liberties. The apparatus is also what made the town worth taking. In 1438, in the long unravelling of the Hundred Years' War, the Castilian captain Rodrigue de Villandrando, a freebooter the chronicles called the Scourge, sacked Treignac to recover a thousand écus that Jean de Comborn had borrowed and not repaid. The market hall was rebuilt in 1484, after the worst was over.[^sack] One does not, as a rule, burn a village to recover a debt unless the village is the kind that can be made to yield one, and Treignac was; the same wealth that raised the bridge invited the man who came to collect.

For all that, the figure the town is proudest of made his name a long way from the granite. Charles Lachaud was born in Treignac in 1817, the son of a local notary, and became, by general consent, the most followed criminal advocate of the Second Empire, the man the newspapers called the King of the Assizes. He defended Marie Lafarge in the arsenic trial that did as much as any case of the century to put toxicology into a French courtroom; he defended Marshal Bazaine after the surrender of Metz, and Gustave Courbet after the column in the Place Vendôme came down, and the murderer Troppmann, whom he could not save from the guillotine.[^lachaud] The Lachaud house survives as the town's museum of Vézère valley life, and it is a pleasing accident of inheritance that the trial lawyer's grandson, Marc Sangnier, the Catholic-democrat who founded the French youth-hostel movement, lies buried in the same Treignac cemetery, two generations of one family pinned to a town neither of them quite lived in.

Treignac is not, I should add for the record, a Plus Beau Village de France; it held that label once and lost it in 2008, and carries instead the quieter Petite Cité de Caractère, which suits it. The most beautiful villages are a club, and Treignac has the air of a place that would rather get on with being itself than mind its standing in one.

The road, meanwhile, has business of its own, and the business is uphill. The Côte de la Croix de Pey begins at the river, or near enough, and the climb out of Treignac is the day's first hard test of the second half, with the Suc au May already three segments behind and the Mont Bessou still to come. There is an argument in the data about how long and how steep this climb is, which I leave to the people whose affair it is; what is not in argument is the road's shape, which is the only thing the rider feels. It tilts up out of the town toward the Plateau and does not relent inside this segment. The cyclist with a memory will recall that in 2020 the Tour came the other way, descending this same road into Treignac, and that it was on this descent that the day's decisive group went clear, Marc Hirschi among them, before he soloed away later for the win. In 2026 the race climbs what it once dropped down. The crest, and the wide plateau country it opens onto, are the next essay's business; this segment owns only the beginning of the ascent, the long lean out of the valley with the gorge dropping away behind.

Our own four riders reach Treignac in their own time, a kilometre or two at a sitting, keeping the honest ledger this blog exists to keep. The opening the last essay promised them has arrived: the trees break, the gorge falls away, the old bridge is there to be crossed. What waits past it is a climb, which is also, by long arrangement, the thing this route has been promising all along. They will take it the way they have taken the rest, on foot and a little at a time.

[^pont]: The Vieux Pont (also called the Pont Médiéval) is documented as early as 1285; the present structure of three broken arches in rubble and dressed granite incorporates twelfth- and thirteenth-century work, and was built to reach the fortified château, since demolished, on its spur above the gorge. It was for roughly six hundred years the only crossing of the Vézère at Treignac, until the Pont Finot (1822) and the Pont Bargy (1840) were built downstream. It was listed a *monument historique* by decree of 1 April 1963 (Mérimée PA00099910, official notice "Pont ancien sur la Vézère à Treignac"). Source: [Pont ancien sur la Vézère à Treignac, Monumentum / base Mérimée PA00099910](https://monumentum.fr/monument-historique/pa00099910/treignac-pont-ancien-sur-la-vezere).

[^kayak]: Treignac hosted the first World Championships of canoe-kayak *descente* (white-water/wild-water racing) on 1-2 August 1959, the founding of the discipline as a world-championship event; the championships returned to the Vézère in 2000 and again in 2022. The dam-fed rapids reach below the town, including the *Saut du Loup*, remains an active white-water course. Source: [Treignac, Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treignac).

[^sack]: Treignac was held through the medieval period by the viscounts of Comborn and received charters of liberties across the period (1205, 1284, 1438). In 1438 the Castilian *routier* Rodrigue de Villandrando, known as *l'Écorcheur* (the Scourge), sacked the town to recover a debt of 1,000 écus owed by Jean de Comborn; the covered market hall (the Halle) was rebuilt in 1484. Source: [Treignac, Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treignac).

[^lachaud]: Charles Lachaud (Treignac, 25 February 1817 - Paris, 9 December 1882), Bonapartist advocate nicknamed the "Roi des Assises," defended Marie Lafarge (the 1840 arsenic-poisoning trial, an early use of forensic toxicology in a French court), Marshal François Achille Bazaine (1873 court-martial), Gustave Courbet (after the demolition of the Vendôme Column), and Jean-Baptiste Troppmann (the 1869 Pantin murders; executed). His grandson Marc Sangnier (1873-1950), founder of *Le Sillon* and of the French youth-hostel movement, is buried in the Treignac cemetery. Sources: [Charles Lachaud, Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Lachaud); [Marc Sangnier, Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marc_Sangnier).
